Science 6 – Quarter 1 Study Guide

Welcome to your Science 6 learning journey! This quarter, you will explore the fascinating world of matter, its classification, and the branches of science. By understanding these concepts, you'll be able to explain what things are made of, how they interact, and how science helps us make sense of our environment.

I. Branches of Science

  • Life Science: Studies living things — plants, animals, and humans. Example: Biology, Zoology
  • Earth Science: Studies Earth's structure, weather, and natural resources. Example: Geology, Meteorology
  • Physical Science: Focuses on non-living matter and energy, including the study of forces, motion, and chemical properties. Example: Chemistry, Physics

Why it matters: These branches help you understand how life functions, how natural disasters happen, and how physical objects behave. This foundation is essential for further learning and everyday problem-solving.

II. Classification of Matter

Matter is anything that occupies space and has mass. It can be classified based on its physical state and composition.

  • Solid: Has a definite shape and volume. Particles are tightly packed.
  • Liquid: Has a definite volume but no definite shape. Particles are less tightly packed.
  • Gas: Has no definite shape or volume. Particles move freely.

Why it matters: Understanding the states of matter helps you observe and explain changes in materials around you, like melting ice or boiling water.

III. Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures

  • Elements: Pure substances that cannot be broken down. Example: Gold, Oxygen
  • Compounds: Made up of two or more elements chemically combined. Example: Water (H₂O)
  • Mixtures: Combinations of substances that are not chemically combined. Example: Salad, Air

Why it matters: Knowing the difference helps you understand chemical reactions and how substances interact.

IV. Solutions, Colloids, and Suspensions

  • Solutions: Homogeneous mixtures where one substance is dissolved in another. Example: Saltwater
  • Colloids: Particles do not settle. Example: Milk, Fog
  • Suspensions: Heterogeneous mixtures where particles settle over time. Example: Muddy water

Why it matters: It helps in making informed decisions about cooking, cleaning, and using everyday products.

V. Metals, Non-metals, and Metalloids

  • Metals: Shiny, good conductors of heat and electricity. Example: Iron, Copper
  • Non-metals: Dull, poor conductors. Example: Oxygen, Carbon
  • Metalloids: Have properties of both metals and non-metals. Example: Silicon

Tip: Metals are used in wires, non-metals in life processes, and metalloids in electronics!

VI. Science in Everyday Life

Science helps us cook, clean, stay healthy, and understand the world. It’s in weather forecasts, medicine, and even the gadgets we use daily.

Why it matters: Science is not just a subject—it’s a tool that helps us solve problems and improve lives.
